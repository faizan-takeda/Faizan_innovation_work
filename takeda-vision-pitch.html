Part I — The Scale of Suffering

Rare Diseases Are Not Rare.
The Suffering Is Invisible.

We call them "rare" but together, they affect more humans than the entire population of the United States. Over 300 million people worldwide are living with one of the 7,000+ identified rare diseases. When you include their families and carers, that number crosses 1 billion lives impacted.

And yet, 95% of these diseases have zero approved treatments. Not one pill. Not one therapy. Nothing. These aren't small numbers in some statistical footnote — this is a humanitarian catastrophe hiding in plain sight.

Part II — The Most Powerful Proof That "Impossible" Is Temporary

15 Years Ago, No One Could Imagine
Money Moving Without Banks.

Consider what India's UPI (Unified Payments Interface) has done to the world of financial access. This isn't theory. This is the most dramatic expansion of a system's reach in human history. And it happened in less than a decade.

🇮🇳 India's UPI: From Zero to Global #1 in 9 Years

August 2016

93,000 transactions in the entire month. Only 35 banks supported it. Regulated, slow, controlled by legacy systems. Cash was king. Less than 6 non-cash transactions per person per year. The idea that a vegetable vendor or a street-side chai stall could accept digital payments was fantasy.

August 2025

20 BILLION transactions in a single month. 641 banks live. ₹24.85 lakh crore processed. UPI now handles more daily transactions than Visa globally — 640+ million per day. 84% of India's digital payments. Active in 7 countries. 49% of the world's entire real-time payment volume. The vegetable vendor now says, "UPI kar do."

Think about this: from 93,000 transactions to 20,000,000,000 per month. That's not 10x growth. That's not 100x. That's over 200,000x growth in 9 years.

Banking was once dominated by physical branches, plastic cards, long queues, and gatekeepers. The idea that a farmer in a remote Indian village could send money with a fingerprint seemed absurd. Today, among households earning less than ₹2 lakh annually, digital payment usage jumped from 12% in 2016 to 78% in 2024 almost entirely because of UPI.

The same revolution happened with smartphones. In 2010, only about 300 million people had smartphones. Today, 5.78 billion people carry one a device more powerful than the computers that sent humans to the moon. The smartphone didn't just become cheaper; the entire ecosystem was redesigned for universal reach.

If a country of 1.4 billion people can go from cash-only to the world's #1 digital payment system in under 10 years why can't we build a system that delivers cures to every rare disease patient on Earth in the next 10?

The parallel is exact. Replace "financial access" with "therapeutic access." Replace "bank branches" with "specialist hospitals." Replace "cash" with "no diagnosis." The infrastructure for universal cure delivery is not a dream it's an engineering problem. And engineering problems have solutions.

Part IV — The Business Case Is Irrefutable

This Isn't Charity.
It's the Biggest Care for our Family and Growth Opportunity in Pharma.

The global orphan drug market is projected to grow from approximately $217 billion in 2025 to over $610 billion by 2034, expanding at a compound annual growth rate of 12.2%. That growth rate consistently outpaces the overall pharmaceutical market (7.5% CAGR for non-orphan drugs).

Orphan drugs are projected to represent 21% of all global prescription drug sales by 2032 — their share has doubled in just one decade. This isn't a niche anymore. This is becoming the center of pharma.

Consider: Over 50% of all new FDA drug approvals in recent years have been orphan drugs. Regulators are actively incentivizing this space with market exclusivity, tax credits, reduced fees, and accelerated review pathways. The system is designed to reward companies who dare to solve rare disease.

And the most powerful proof? Gene therapies are now turning lifetime treatment regimens into single-dose cures. Fifteen gene therapies gained FDA approval in 2024–2025 alone. The cost of genome sequencing has fallen 99% in 15 years. AI is identifying drug targets in months, not decades. The tools exist. The economics work. The regulatory winds are favorable.
